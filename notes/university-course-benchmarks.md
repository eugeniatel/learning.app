# University course benchmarks

These are reference structures, not obligations. The app curriculum should stay lighter than a university course and should preserve the rule that weeks move with the learner.

## LLM and AI systems

Primary anchors:

- Stanford CS336, Language Modeling from Scratch.
- Stanford CS25, Transformers United.
- MIT OpenCourseWare deep learning and language-model lectures where useful.

Curriculum translation:

- Architecture first: tokenization, transformer block, attention, residual stream, MLPs, positional encodings.
- Then systems and scaling: GPUs, kernels, parallelism, inference, scaling laws.
- Then data and evals: data sources, filtering, deduplication, evaluation.
- Then post-training: SFT, preference learning, RLHF/RLVR, reasoning models.
- Interpretability runs as a parallel strand, not something delayed until the end.

Local pacing:

- One concept per week.
- 90 percent reading and notes, 10 percent small synthesis or sketch.
- No from-scratch implementation unless the week explicitly calls for it.

## DeFi and protocol risk

Primary anchors:

- Stanford CS251, Blockchain Technologies.
- Stanford EE374, Fundamentals of Blockchain Infrastructure.
- MIT Blockchain and Money.
- MIT Cryptocurrency Engineering and Design.

Curriculum translation:

- Use university courses only for primitives and structure; they are not deep enough for the working DeFi context.
- Focus the DeFi track on stablecoins, lending protocols, liquidations, DEXs, MEV, governance, and protocol-specific failure modes.
- Use protocol case studies rather than abstract topic blocks: Morpho first, Euler second, then Silo and adjacent lending/stablecoin systems.
- Every lending protocol gets the same practitioner lens: primitive, market structure, risk parameters, oracle path, liquidation path, bad-debt path, external dependencies, stress scenario, and protocol comparison.
- Primary depth should come from protocol docs, lite papers, audits, dashboards, governance/forum posts, risk disclosures, liquidation research, and incident writeups.

Local pacing:

- One protocol or mechanism per week.
- End each week with a mechanism note, not a full report.

## Dr. Zhivago

Primary anchors:

- Yale Slavic Languages and Literatures emphasis on Russian literature, culture, literary analysis, and historical/social context.
- Yale Russian/Russian Studies requirements combine literature in translation with Russian, East European, and Eurasian history or social science.
- Yale Slavic graduate guidance treats reading lists as a start, not a fixed canon, and expects readers to build context around their interests.

Curriculum translation:

- One part of the novel at a time, but reading itself is assumed and should not be framed as the main app task.
- The app acts as a taught companion: context brief, reference notes, character map, scene lens, required secondary source, and one small question.
- Context stays attached to the part being read: Russian history, class, religion, revolution, literary form, and Pasternak's perspective.
- Every week includes one article, academic paper, or essay to expand the reading.
- Notes should enrich reading rather than turn the novel into a survey course.

Local pacing:

- One part per week when it fits.
- Each part gets one scene, one reference, one question, and one perspective note.
